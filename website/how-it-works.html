How it works

The safety graph, explained

Safe Walk builds a scored safety layer on top of Google Maps by combining municipal open data, community reports, and time-aware scoring into a single API that any platform can query.

Architecture

How a route request flows through the system

When you request a route, Safe Walk calls the Google Routes API with computeAlternativeRoutes: true to get up to three different walking paths. Each path comes back as an encoded polyline — a compressed list of lat/lng points. We decode, sample, score, and rank them.

We decode each polyline, sample points along the route at regular intervals, and score every sampled point against our safety graph. The route with the lowest average danger score becomes the "safest" recommendation. The route Google ranks first is labeled "fastest." You see both.

Safety scoring engine

Three signals, one score

Every point on every route gets a safety cost between 0 (very safe) and 1 (very dangerous). The cost is computed from three signals:

Signal 01 / incident density

Nearby incidents decay by distance, time, and severity

We query all police incidents within 500 metres of the point. Each incident's contribution is weighted by three factors multiplied together:

distance_weight = 1 / (1 + metres / 100)An incident 50m away contributes 3x more than one at 300m.
recency_weight = e-days_old / 365Last month's incident counts heavily. Two years ago is nearly zero.
severity_weight =
Assault / Robbery / Sexual Violation → 1.0
Break and Enter → 0.5
Theft → 0.3 Auto Theft → 0.2
incident_contribution =
distance_weight × recency_weight × severity_weight
risk decays with distance
Signal 02 / streetlight coverage

Lighting is treated as infrastructure, not vibes

We count streetlights within 100 metres of each point. Five or more nearby streetlights means the area is well-lit and the lighting danger score drops to zero. Zero streetlights means maximum lighting danger. This layer is weighted heavily after dark and almost ignored during daytime.

lights_nearby = count of streetlights within 100m
lighting_score = max(0, 1 − lights_nearby / 5)0 = well-lit, 1 = no lights. Clamped at 0.

Signal 03 / time of day

The model changes after dark

The scoring engine knows what time it is. During the day (7 AM to 7 PM), incident history carries most of the weight. After dark (9 PM to 5 AM), streetlight coverage becomes much more important. Dawn and dusk hours interpolate smoothly between the two modes, following Toronto's actual patterns.

The route's overall safety score is the average of all sampled points along the path. A route scoring 0.15 is meaningfully safer than one scoring 0.40, even though both numbers are below 0.5. The comparison between routes is what matters — and when you see both routes on the map, the difference is usually obvious: the safe route sticks to well-lit main streets while the fast route cuts through darker, higher-incident areas.

Data sources

Where the data comes from

Toronto Police Service, Major Crime Indicators

Every geocoded assault, robbery, break-and-enter, theft, and sexual violation. Updated regularly via the ArcGIS feature service at data.torontopolice.on.ca. Tens of thousands of records covering multiple years.

City of Toronto, Street Lighting

The location of every streetlight in the city — approximately 160,000 points. Sourced from Toronto's Open Data portal via CKAN API. Includes type and wattage where available.

Community reports

User-submitted reports of harassment, poor lighting, suspicious activity, and other safety concerns. Weighted lower than police data initially, but gain weight with corroboration from multiple users.

Google Routes API

Walking route geometry and alternatives. We use Google for what it's good at — pathfinding on the road network — and add what it's missing: safety scoring.

API reference

Two useful surfaces. Both return JSON.

The main integration is the route endpoint. The GeoJSON endpoints expose the layers that power map overlays and partner dashboards.

POST /api/route

Returns the fastest and safest walking routes between two addresses.

// POST /api/route
{
  "origin": "Robarts Library, Toronto",
  "destination": "100 Harbord St, Toronto"
}
{
  "fastest": {
    "polyline": "encoded_polyline",
    "distanceMeters": 1200,
    "duration": "900s",
    "safety_score": 0.42
  },
  "safest": {
    "safety_score": 0.18
  }
}

What's next

The roadmap

The current scoring engine uses statistical weighting. The next version adds an AI prediction layer using Claude to estimate risk in areas with sparse police data, based on patterns from nearby areas: time clustering, proximity to certain location types, and historical incident trajectories.

We're also expanding the community reporting layer and building integrations for university campus safety apps, starting with University of Toronto, TMU, and York.
